Language Models are Few-Shot Learners

Tom B. Brown, Benjamin Mann, Nick Ryder, Melanie Subbiah, Jared Kaplan, Prafulla Dhariwal, Arvind Neelakantan, Pranav Shyam, Girish Sastry, Amanda Askell, Sandhini Agarwal, Ariel Herbert-Voss, Gretchen Krueger, Tom Henighan, Rewon Child, Aditya Ramesh, Daniel M. Ziegler, Jeffrey Wu, Clemens Winter, Christopher Hesse, Mark Chen, Eric Sigler, Mateusz Litwin, Scott Gray, Benjamin Chess, Jack Clark, Christopher Berner, Sam McCandlish, Alec Radford, Ilya Sutskever, Dario Amodei (2020) — arXiv:2005.14165

What this paper claims

  • Scaling language models to 175 billion parameters substantially improves zero-shot, one-shot, and few-shot performance on NLP benchmarks without any gradient updates or fine-tuning.
  • GPT-3 few-shot performance is sometimes competitive with or surpasses state-of-the-art fine-tuned models on certain tasks such as TriviaQA closed-book and translation into English.
  • The gap between zero-shot, one-shot, and few-shot performance generally grows with model capacity, suggesting larger models are more proficient in-context learners.
  • GPT-3 can generate synthetic news articles of approximately 200 words that human evaluators can only distinguish from real articles at near-chance accuracy (~52%).
  • GPT-3 struggles on comparison-style NLI tasks (WiC, ANLI) even in the few-shot setting, performing near random chance on some.
  • Cross-entropy loss and downstream task performance follow a smooth power-law trend as a function of model compute, extending over three orders of magnitude.
