Language Models are Few-Shot Learners

Tom B. Brown, Benjamin Mann, Nick Ryder, Melanie Subbiah, Jared Kaplan, Prafulla Dhariwal, Arvind Neelakantan, Pranav Shyam, Girish Sastry, Amanda Askell, Sandhini Agarwal, Ariel Herbert-Voss, Gretchen Krueger, Tom Henighan, Rewon Child, Aditya Ramesh, Daniel M. Ziegler, Jeffrey Wu, Clemens Winter, Christopher Hesse, Mark Chen, Eric Sigler, Mateusz Litwin, Scott Gray, Benjamin Chess, Jack Clark, Christopher Berner, Sam McCandlish, Alec Radford, Ilya Sutskever, Dario Amodei (2020) — arXiv:2005.14165

What this paper claims

  • Scaling language models to 175 billion parameters substantially improves few-shot, one-shot, and zero-shot task performance without any gradient updates or fine-tuning.
  • GPT-3 few-shot performance is sometimes competitive with or surpasses state-of-the-art fine-tuned models on certain benchmarks.
  • The gap between zero-shot, one-shot, and few-shot performance generally grows with model capacity, suggesting larger models are more proficient in-context learners.
  • Human evaluators can barely distinguish GPT-3 175B generated news articles from human-written articles, with detection accuracy near chance at approximately 52%.
  • GPT-3 struggles on tasks requiring comparison of two sentences or snippets, such as natural language inference and word-in-context tasks.
